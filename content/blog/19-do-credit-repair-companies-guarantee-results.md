If you've ever been told by a credit-repair company that they can guarantee a 100-point score increase, or that you'll definitely qualify for a mortgage in 60 days — you should walk away. Not because the result is impossible, but because **the guarantee itself is illegal.**

This is not a marketing position. It's federal law.

## The Credit Repair Organizations Act, in plain English

The Credit Repair Organizations Act — CROA, codified at 15 U.S.C. § 1679 — is the federal statute that governs every credit-repair company in the United States. Section 404 of CROA prohibits a credit-repair organization from making any statement, "directly or by implication, with respect to a consumer's credit-worthiness, credit-standing, or credit-capacity that is untrue or misleading."

In practice, that means an operator cannot tell you:

- "You will qualify for a mortgage after our program."
- "We guarantee a specific number of items removed from your credit report."
- "Your score will improve by X points."
- "We will get any item off your report you don't want there."

A company that makes those claims has already broken the law before you've signed anything.

## What CAN a legitimate operator say?

The honest version sounds like this: *"We'll dispute every inaccurate or unverifiable item on your report under FCRA §611 and §623. The bureaus and the data furnishers are required to investigate within 30 days. Most clients see meaningful changes in 60–90 days, but results vary by file."*

Notice what's there, and what isn't:

- ✅ Specific legal mechanism (FCRA dispute process)
- ✅ Concrete timeline based on statute (30-day investigation requirement)
- ✅ Average duration based on internal data
- ❌ No promise of a specific outcome
- ❌ No promise of qualification for any specific loan
- ❌ No promise of any score-point lift

That's CROA-compliant copy. It's also more useful to you than a guarantee — because the guarantee was never going to be honored anyway.

## Why does this matter for your wallet?

CROA also requires that fees be charged based on services actually performed. Section 404(b) prohibits accepting payment for services *before* those services are fully performed. The FTC's Telemarketing Sales Rule (TSR §310.4(a)(2)) layers an additional restriction in any case where the company is reached via telephone.

Translation: an operator that demands $1,500 upfront, before any disputes have been filed and any results have been documented, is operating outside CROA's fee structure.

The compliant model — and the one Clean Path Credit operates on — is **per-round-after-completion**: you pay for each round of dispute work after that round is documented and delivered to you, not before.

## What about "money-back guarantees"?

A money-back guarantee is different from an outcome guarantee. A company can legally offer to refund their fees if their service doesn't meet the terms of the contract. That's a contractual remedy, not a statement about your credit standing.

Two things to watch for:

1. **Read the conditions.** A money-back guarantee that requires you to wait 12 months, file a written claim with notarized signatures, and prove you followed every recommendation is functionally not a refund.
2. **Distinguish refund from outcome.** "We refund if we don't remove X items" is still an outcome promise. "We refund if we don't perform the dispute work we agreed to" is a service guarantee, which is fine.

## What this means for your decision

If you're vetting a credit-repair company right now, the cleanest test is this: ask them, on a recorded call or in writing, *"Do you guarantee any specific outcome with my credit?"*

- If they say "yes, we guarantee X" — they've just broken CROA. Walk away.
- If they say "no, we can't guarantee outcomes, but here's what we can do" — they're operating in compliance. Now evaluate the service on its merits.

It is genuinely unusual for an industry's most damning sales objection ("you can't even guarantee anything!") to also be the strongest signal of operator quality. But that's exactly the situation in credit repair.

The companies that promise the most are the ones that will help you the least.
