## Chapter 1 — The 2026 Credit Revolution

For 30+ years, the rules of mortgage credit scoring favored one type of borrower: the person who opened a credit card at 22 and used it consistently. That person built a "thick file." Everyone else — working-class buyers, immigrants, ITIN borrowers, lifelong renters — was effectively penalized for not playing a game they'd never been told existed.

That changed in 2025.

The **Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA)** approved two new scoring models for use by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the two largest mortgage lenders in the country: **FICO 10T** and **VantageScore 4.0**. They're being phased in across 2025-2026, and they include something the old models never did: **rent payments, utility bills, and BNPL (buy-now-pay-later) data**.

What this means in plain English:

- Your **rent** — if reported correctly — now counts as credit history.
- Your **electric bill, water bill, internet bill** — if you've been paying them on time — now count.
- Your **Klarna, Afterpay, or Affirm** history now counts.

For someone who has been paying rent on time for 5+ years but has never had a credit card, this is the difference between *credit invisible* (no score) and *scoreable for a mortgage*. That's the entire ballgame.

### The numbers behind why this matters

- **24% of US consumers** skipped or underpaid a credit obligation in the last 12 months. *(FICO Spring 2026 Score Insights)*
- **The average FICO score is 714**, down from 716 a year ago. *(FICO Spring 2026)*
- **25.3 million US adults — 9.8% of all adults — have no scorable credit record at all.** Most are immigrants, working-class earners with cash income, or younger first-time buyers. *(CFPB Office of Research, June 2025)*
- **Mortgage delinquencies are up 58% since 2021.** *(FICO Spring 2026)* — lenders are getting more cautious overall, but they're also more open to using new scoring models that catch qualified buyers the old models missed.

Translation: there are more credit-challenged Americans right now than there have been in decades, AND there are new tools available that didn't exist a year ago. The opportunity is real.

But you need to know how to use it.

## Chapter 2 — Where You Are: A Self-Audit

Before you can fix your credit, you need to know what's actually on your credit reports. Most people skip this step — which is why most "credit repair" attempts fail.

### Step 1: Pull all three credit reports (free)

Go to **annualcreditreport.com**. This is the only federally-authorized free credit-report site. Other sites that say "free credit report" usually want you to sign up for a paid monitoring service.

Pull reports from all three bureaus:

- Equifax
- Experian
- TransUnion

Each bureau's report is slightly different. Items that show up on one bureau might not show on another. You need to see all three.

### Step 2: Run the 12-item review

For each report, look for these 12 categories. Print this checklist or screenshot it on your phone:

**1. Personal information**
  - Wrong name spellings or aliases?
  - Old addresses you don't recognize?
  - Wrong date of birth or SSN last 4?

**2. Payment history (35% of FICO score)**
  - Late payments (30, 60, 90, 120+ days late)
  - Are any inaccurate?
  - Are any older than 7 years?

**3. Collections**
  - How many?
  - Total dollar amount?
  - Original creditor identified?
  - Date of first delinquency on each?

**4. Charge-offs**
  - Account name and date?
  - Cross-check: some may be the same account that became a collection — don't get charged twice for the same debt.

**5. Credit utilization (30% of FICO score)**
  - For each open card: balance ÷ credit limit. Goal: under 30%.
  - Lower is better.

**6. Length of credit history (15% of FICO score)**
  - Average age of accounts. Older = better.

**7. Hard inquiries (10% of FICO score)**
  - New ones from the last 24 months
  - Any you didn't authorize?

**8. New credit (10% of FICO score)**
  - Accounts opened in the last 12 months

**9. Bankruptcies**
  - Chapter 7 stays 10 years
  - Chapter 13 stays 7 years

**10. Tax liens / judgments**
  - Most no longer report on credit per the National Consumer Assistance Plan, but verify anyway.

**11. Medical bills in collections**
  - Per the 2022 changes, **medical debt under $500 should NOT be reported.** If you see one, it's disputable.
  - **Paid medical collections should NOT be reported either.** Also disputable.

**12. Already-disputed items**
  - Look for "consumer disputes this account" notations. These tell you what was disputed previously and how it resolved.

If you can answer all 12 of these for each bureau, you've done what 90% of consumers haven't. You now have a baseline.

## Chapter 3 — Of Those 12 Things, Which Are Worth Disputing?

Not everything bad on your report is disputable. Here's the simple test:

### Disputable (under FCRA §611 and §623)

- **Inaccurate information** — wrong dates, wrong amounts, wrong account ownership
- **Information older than the legal reporting limit** — 7 years for most negatives, 10 for Chapter 7 bankruptcy
- **Items you don't recognize** — potentially identity theft
- **Medical collections under $500** — should not be reporting since 2022
- **Paid medical collections** — should not be reporting since 2023
- **Items where the bureau or furnisher can't prove the data is accurate**
- **Mixed file errors** — someone else's data on your report (common with similar names)

### NOT disputable (just because you don't like it)

- Accurate late payments you actually missed
- Accurate collections from accounts that are actually yours
- Accurate hard inquiries you authorized
- High utilization on cards (this is a *usage* issue, not a *dispute* issue)

Many "credit repair" companies disappear because they file bulk frivolous disputes on accurate items. The bureau initially deletes some (because furnishers don't respond in 30 days), but the items pop back next month when the furnisher confirms accuracy. Net result: customer paid $1,500-3,000 for a temporary score bump that disappeared. That's the bait-and-switch pattern that gives the industry a bad name.

A real credit-readiness operation files disputes only on items that are actually disputable under FCRA. Slower, but the deletions hold.

## Chapter 4 — FICO 10T & VantageScore 4.0 (The 2026 Game-Changer)

OK, back to the new scoring models.

Here's what changed:

| What | Old (FICO 8 / VantageScore 3) | New (FICO 10T / VantageScore 4.0) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rent payments | Ignored | Counted as credit history |
| Utility bills | Ignored | Counted (electric, water, internet, phone) |
| BNPL (Klarna, Afterpay) | Ignored | Counted |
| Medical debt | Heavy penalty | Reduced penalty |
| Trended data (24-month behavior) | Ignored | Counted (rewards declining balances) |

For mortgage applications, lenders using FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0 will see a **higher score for most rent-paying first-time buyers** than the old models would have produced.

But here's the catch: **your rent only counts if it's reported.**

Most landlords don't report rent to bureaus. To get your rent on your credit history, you typically need a third-party service:

- **Esusu** — partners with many landlords; $0-10/month
- **Boom** — direct-to-consumer; $4-7/month
- **RentReporters** — direct-to-consumer; $9.95/month + $50 signup
- **CreditMyRent** — newer; $5-8/month

These services report your rent payments to one or more bureaus. Most offer **backdated reporting** (24 months of historical rent payments retroactively added) for an additional fee — often the single highest-leverage move you can make for a thin file.

Same applies to utilities through services like **Experian Boost** (free, links to your bank account, picks up utility/streaming/phone payments).

**This isn't credit repair. This is credit construction.** And it's probably the lowest-effort, highest-impact thing you can do this month.

## Chapter 5 — The 90-Day Mortgage-Readiness Calendar

If you want to be mortgage-ready in 90 days, here's a realistic week-by-week timeline.

### Days 1-7: Audit and setup
  - [ ] Pull all 3 credit reports from annualcreditreport.com
  - [ ] Complete the 12-item self-audit (Chapter 2)
  - [ ] Sign up for Esusu, Boom, or RentReporters (with backdated reporting)
  - [ ] Sign up for Experian Boost (free; picks up utilities)
  - [ ] Identify 1-3 items on your report that are clearly disputable

### Days 8-21: Disputes go out (Round 1)
  - [ ] Use the FCRA §611 template (Chapter 6) for bureau-to-bureau disputes
  - [ ] Use the FCRA §623 template for direct-to-furnisher disputes
  - [ ] Mail certified, return-receipt — keep proof of mailing
  - [ ] Mark your calendar: federal law says bureaus have 30 days to respond

### Days 22-35: Wait for responses
  - [ ] Don't open new credit during this window
  - [ ] Don't close old accounts (closing reduces avg account age)
  - [ ] DO continue paying every account on time
  - [ ] DO start paying down high-utilization cards (target: under 30%)

### Days 36-50: Round 2 disputes
  - [ ] Bureau responses arrive — review carefully
  - [ ] For items where the bureau "verified" but you have new evidence, file a re-dispute with the new info
  - [ ] For items where the bureau didn't respond on time, the items must be deleted by law

### Days 51-65: Apply for a secured card if needed
  - [ ] If your "thin file" still flags, apply for a secured credit card
  - [ ] Recommended: Discover It Secured, Capital One Platinum Secured
  - [ ] Use it for one purchase per month, pay in full
  - [ ] Don't apply for more than one new card

### Days 66-80: Round 3 (if items remain)
  - [ ] Final round of disputes on remaining items
  - [ ] Verify rent reporting is showing on your credit report
  - [ ] Pull updated credit reports — note the score change

### Days 81-90: Mortgage application prep
  - [ ] Updated credit reports in hand
  - [ ] Documentation gathered (W-2s, tax returns, bank statements)
  - [ ] Pre-qualification letter with a mortgage LO
  - [ ] If income is non-traditional (cash, ITIN, multiple jobs), find a LO who specializes in those

This is a real 90-day plan. Realistic. CROA-compliant. **No guarantees on outcome** — your specific timeline depends on what's on your file, how many bureaus, and how creditors respond.

## Chapter 6 — Two Free Dispute Letter Templates

These templates are for educational use. They're based on language from the FTC and CFPB consumer guides. Adapt to your specific situation.

### Template 1: FCRA §611 — Dispute to a Credit Bureau

```
[Your Name]
[Your Current Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Bureau Name — Equifax / Experian / TransUnion]
[Bureau's Dispute Address — see annualcreditreport.com for current addresses]

Re: Dispute of inaccurate information on my credit report
SSN (last 4): ____
Date of Birth: ____

Dear Sir or Madam:

I am writing to dispute the following item(s) on my credit report:

  Account Name: _______
  Account Number (last 4): _______

  Reason for dispute (choose one or more):
    • This account does not belong to me
    • The balance is incorrect
    • The status (paid/closed/late) is incorrect
    • The date of first delinquency is incorrect
    • This account is older than 7 years and should be removed
    • I have no record of this account

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) §611, you are required to
investigate this disputed item within 30 days and to remove or correct
any information that cannot be verified.

Please send me a written response with the results of your investigation.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

Enclosures: [If applicable: copy of identification, proof of address, copy
of the credit report with the disputed item highlighted]
```

### Template 2: FCRA §623 — Dispute Direct to Furnisher

```
[Your Name]
[Your Current Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Furnisher / Creditor Name]
[Furnisher's Customer Dispute Address — usually on your statement, or at
consumerfinance.gov]

Re: Dispute of inaccurate information being reported to credit bureaus
Account Number (last 4): _______
SSN (last 4): _______

Dear Sir or Madam:

I am writing to dispute the accuracy of information you are reporting to
the credit bureaus regarding the above account.

Specifically, I dispute the following:

  [Describe the inaccuracy in detail. Include dates, amounts, and any
  documentation you have.]

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) §623, you are required to
investigate this dispute and to correct or remove the inaccurate
information from the credit bureaus' records if it cannot be verified.

Please send me a written response and notify all three credit bureaus
(Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) of the correction.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

Enclosures: [If applicable]
```

### Important notes on using these templates

- **Mail certified with return receipt** — this proves the bureau or furnisher received it.
- **Keep copies** of everything you send.
- **Don't include sensitive info** like full SSN or full account number.
- **Include proof of identity** (driver's license + utility bill) per the bureau's standard requirements.
- Both bureaus AND furnishers have **30 days** to respond. If they don't, the disputed information must be deleted by law.

These templates work for items that are **actually disputable**. Don't use them on accurate negative information — bureaus will verify and the dispute will be denied.

## Chapter 7 — Five Myths Credit-Repair Companies Tell You

If you've ever called a "credit repair" number from a Spanish-language radio ad or seen a billboard promising "remove bad credit in 30 days," you've heard these myths:

### Myth 1: "We can remove anything from your credit report — even accurate items."

**Truth:** No, they can't. Or rather, they can temporarily — but the items pop back when furnishers re-confirm. CROA §404(a)(3) makes it ILLEGAL for credit-repair companies to make this promise.

### Myth 2: "Pay us $2,000 upfront and we'll guarantee a 100-point boost."

**Truth:** Charging upfront fees for credit repair is **ILLEGAL** under federal law (CROA §404(b)). Period. Any company that demands upfront payment is breaking the law. Specific score guarantees are also prohibited.

### Myth 3: "Once you sign with us, you can't cancel."

**Truth:** You have an **UNCONDITIONAL 3-day right to cancel** any credit-repair contract under CROA §405(a)(2). No questions. No penalty.

### Myth 4: "We'll handle everything — you don't need to do anything."

**Truth:** Real credit improvement requires YOUR participation. You need to keep paying your accounts on time. You need to sign authorizations. You need to provide documentation. Any company that tells you to "just relax" while they "work their magic" is selling magic, not credit work.

### Myth 5: "Closing your old credit cards will boost your score."

**Truth:** The opposite is usually true. Closing accounts reduces your average account age (which counts toward your score) and reduces your total available credit (which raises your utilization). Almost always: keep old cards open, even if you don't use them.

If a company tells you any of these myths, walk away. They're either uninformed or actively misleading you.

## Chapter 8 — ITIN, Cash Income, and Mixed-Status Families

For Latino working-class buyers and many other non-traditional-income earners, the conventional mortgage path is full of locked doors. Here's what's actually possible.

### ITIN borrowers

**Yes, you can get a mortgage with an ITIN.** There are dedicated ITIN mortgage programs from non-bank lenders. They typically:

- Require 20% down (vs. 3.5% for FHA conventional)
- Have higher interest rates than conventional (1-3 percentage points)
- Require 2 years of tax returns showing reportable income
- **Some allow rent payment history as a factor** — huge benefit with FICO 10T

The key: most LOs don't know these programs exist or only know one. Find a LO who **specializes** in ITIN mortgages — usually independent brokers serving Latino markets.

### Cash income / multiple jobs

If you earn cash (jardinero, mecánico, vendedor, restaurant work, side jobs), there are three paths:

1. **Bank statement loans** — lender looks at 12-24 months of bank deposits. Your cash, deposited regularly, counts.
2. **Tax returns** — if you've reported the cash income on Schedule C or 1099, that documented income is conventional.
3. **Profit and loss letter (CPA)** — for self-employed earners, a CPA-signed P&L can be used in some programs.

The single most important step: **deposit your cash into a bank account regularly.** Not after the fact. Not in lump sums. Regular weekly or bi-weekly deposits look like income to a lender.

### Mixed-status families

If your household has people with both SSNs and ITINs, programs exist that accept both:

- Mortgages can have multiple borrowers — your spouse with a SSN + you with an ITIN can be on the same loan.
- Income from non-borrower household members can sometimes be counted for qualifying purposes (depends on the program).
- Some programs explicitly serve "blended status" families.

The work isn't credit repair. It's matching the right program to your situation. Most LOs default to conventional loans because that's what they know. A program-savvy LO opens doors.

## Chapter 10 — When to DIY vs When to Hire Help

Here's the honest math.

### DIY makes sense if:
  - You have time (5-10 hours/month for 6+ months)
  - Your situation is simple (1-3 items to dispute, no complex credit history)
  - You're comfortable with paperwork, certified mail, and waiting periods
  - You're disciplined about paying bills on time
  - You want to learn the process

### Hire a credit-readiness operation if:
  - You have a complex file (5+ disputable items, multiple bureaus, old debt)
  - You don't have time or attention to manage 90+ days of structured work
  - You're applying for a mortgage in a specific timeline window
  - You have non-traditional income (ITIN, cash, mixed-status) and need LO matchmaking too
  - You want bilingual support
  - You want weekly written progress reports without managing the work yourself

### What does a CROA-compliant credit-readiness operation actually do?

Read this list and ask yourself if you'd rather DIY:

  - Pulls and analyzes all three reports for disputable items
  - Drafts custom dispute letters (not generic templates)
  - Mails certified, tracks responses, manages 30-day windows
  - Adjusts strategy round-to-round based on responses
  - Sets up rent reporting (Esusu, Boom, RentReporters)
  - Coordinates with mortgage LOs, including ITIN-specialized ones
  - Provides weekly written progress reports
  - Handles bilingual delivery if needed
  - Prepares files specifically for FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0
  - Documents the case for compliance / audit
  - **Charges only after demonstrated outcomes** (CROA §404(b))

If that list is "I want someone else to do this," hiring help saves you 6-12 months and a lot of stress. If that list is "I can do all of this on my own," DIY is fine.

There's no shame in hiring help. There's also no shame in DIY. Just don't pay anyone upfront, don't sign anything you can't cancel within 3 days, and don't believe specific score guarantees.

## Bonus: 90-Day Budget Template (Mortgage-Ready)

Getting mortgage-ready isn't only about credit. Lenders want to see savings momentum and stable cash flow. Here's a simple framework:

### Income (after taxes)
$_______ / month

### Fixed expenses
  - Rent: $_______
  - Car payment: $_______
  - Insurance (auto + health): $_______
  - Utilities (electric + water + internet): $_______
  - Phone: $_______
  - Subscriptions: $_______

**Subtotal fixed:** $_______

### Variable expenses (monthly average)
  - Groceries: $_______
  - Gas: $_______
  - Eating out: $_______
  - Entertainment: $_______
  - Personal care: $_______
  - Other: $_______

**Subtotal variable:** $_______

### Goals (the next 90 days)
  - Down payment savings (target: 1-3% of home price): $_______ / month
  - Credit card paydowns (target: under 30% utilization): $_______ / month
  - Emergency fund (target: 3 months of expenses): $_______ / month

**Subtotal goals:** $_______

### Total monthly outflow
$_______ (should be less than your income)

$_______

If your slack is negative, you'll need to either increase income or reduce variable expenses for the next 90 days. Mortgage lenders don't just look at credit — they look at cash flow, debt-to-income ratio, and savings history. All three matter.
